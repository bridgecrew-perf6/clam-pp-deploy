A Foodstand Clam

A web game based on running a food stand on Maple Island. A fun project with the purpose of learning to code in vanilla Javascript. Built from scratch.

LIVE DEMO SOURCE CODE
Tech Stack
  • Built with Javascript, HTML, and CSS
  • Version control and deployment with Git
Technical Achievments
  • Created gameloop with a stabilized frame rate of 100fps on all computers/browsers. Did this by implementing a gameloop which ingests the current timestamp, and checks the difference between the last timestamp. If the change in time is >= 10 milliseconds, the next frame is updated. As a new frame is loaded 100 times a second, the game framerate is cemented at 100 frames per second regardless of the user's browser speed.

  • Implemented a simple gamestate design pattern to manage the different phases of the game. At any given time, one gamestate is active (like a Pause gamestate, Menu gamestate, etc.), and going to other gamestates changes elements like the background image, active functions, etc.

Ok. First I'll answer the question: Who am I?

I'm a 20-something year old tech professional, born and raised in Vancouver, Canada, looking to find work as a developer.





Now that's a longer story...

I suppose I don't have the traditional background of a developer. No comp. sci. degree or development bootcamp here.

But technology has been a big part of my life for a long time.

And I think the best place to start, is from the start...





A Geeky Beginning

In my youth, there were a few landmark moments that predisposed me to a career in technology. and predisposed me to being a nerdy guy in general

First was around the age of 10, when my parents brought home a Windows Vista PC.

It was heavy, beige, and was strictly for "homework only". It wasn't event connected to the internet.

But that didn't stop me from spending hours playing with Space Pinball, Minesweeper, and making custom Starcraft maps.

A typical Windows PC in the 2000's, just like the one my parents picked up in 2006

The next landmark moment was during high school. I got my own computer with wifi connection, and I could not get enough of it Just ask my parents .
Eventually it led me into my first internet-acquired skill: PC building.

When I was 15, with the power of Google, Youtube, and Reddit, I ordered parts and successfully built my first custom PC. When I saw it spin to life for the first time, I realized something huge:

I proved to myself that, with just a computer, the internet, and some good 'ol dedication, I could learn real, practical skills. For free.





Yep!

Through my later high school years, continued my self-studies in technology and computing. I helped people with their computer problems, learned to build websites Solely with Wordpress. Thinking back on those websites makes me cringe-- they were bad., and even dabbled in programming. Though nothing ever came of it; just learned some python syntax and made some ultra-basic projects


But by the time graduation rolled around and it was time to choose my next steps, I didn't even consider studying Computer Science. Instead, I elected to study business.





"Wait, what? You went to business school?"

Yep.

My mentor I had in my youth (and still have to this day) was an entrepreneur, and many of my role models were business-people, so it felt very natural for me to go to business school.

And although I was a shy student, I figured it'd help me open up more, and gain social confidence. It did. But, not going to lie, it was really difficult being in business school as an introvert / socially anxious person. However, I'm glad I did it, and there's no question it made me more socially capable,

I had my eyes set on the Business Technology Management major at UBC's prestigious Sauder business school. Here was the program for me, I thought. I can study business and technology concurrently. Perfect.

The iconic Sauder building at UBC. The dream school of many money-hungry BC school kids

But I didn't make the grades to get in. I was dissapointed to say the least. Instead, I went to Capilano University for my first year.

A Capilano U hoodie. I wanted to buy one, but I was a stereotypical broke student at the time.




It felt terrible. I didn't give up though. I studied like mad. Had a great semester. Kept studying like mad. Had another great semester.

I somehow managed to exceed the extremely competitive grades required for a transfer. For my second year, I was admitted to UBC Sauder, and entered the Business Technology Management concentration.

This was another landmark moment for me. It was perhaps the most impactful lesson I've had in the value of grit, dedication, and focus.

University Days

University wasn't cheap, so I was determined to make the most out of it, career-wise. I jumped into the university's tech scene, joining clubs, running events, and pursuing projects.

In 2017, my work paid off. I was lucky enough to land an 8-month internship at Appnovation Technologies in the New Business (Presales) department.

It felt like my big break. I was working hard to build a career in tech and business, and here it was: my first foray into a real, mature tech company.





My company supplied laptop and a plate from the weekly office lunch.
For a 20 year old me, this was making it.

It confirmed my suspicions that I'd enjoy working in the ever-dynamic technology industry. I liked it so much I stayed for a full year, putting in part-time hours while going to classes.

As much as I loved this taste of working in a tech company, though, I didn't find pre-sales particularly interesting. I figured I'd try a different role for my next internship.

Come 2018, I switched things up, entering a summer internship as a Jr. Business Analyst at VCH, for BC's Corporate Systems Transformations project.

The nametag I needed to get into the CST office.
Working in this healthcare tech project was a stark contrast to the laid-back, pool-table, Friday beer keg vibe at Appnovation

Though I didn't mind the Business Analyst work, the healthcare vertical didn't particularly excite me. And although it was a technology project, things were very departmentalized, so I was far away from the development side of things.





Reflecting on these internships, my favorite challenges in both of these roles was when I was either:

1. Fixing something (usually IT-related), OR

2. Building something (usually a Powerpoint slide-deck At appno, or an Excel dashboard)

I realized I was a much better technologist than a business-person. And I enjoyed the IT side of things more, too.

This was an invaluable lesson as the time creeped inevitably forward, and I was getting closer to my Bachelor's degree graduation.

Out of University, Into the Workforce
 My lenovo thinkpad I used throughout University. I probably had the oldest laptop among my classmates, but I loved it.
As a student, sticker-bombing your laptop is a must.

Come 2019, it was my year to graduate.

I fired out many, many resumes, as university graduates do.

The applications turned to interviews, which then turned to offers. It was time to choose-- what would I do for a living?






I knew I wanted to work in tech. And at the time, I was working at my favorite role I've had yet: a part-time IT help desk role on campus.

This help-desk role made me super user-conscious. Time and time again, it showed me how good it felt to work with technology, solve problems, and help users.

When I was lucky enough to receive an offer from Visier Technologies for a full-time role, providing technical support for their SAAS offerings, I decided this was the best next step for me and my career in tech.

I could learn all the things working in an innovative, high-growth SAAS company has to teach.

After graduating, I joined them as a full-time Technical Support Analyst.

My first permanent, full-time, grown-up job. I made it. I could finally call myself a technology professional. Another landmark moment.







The start of a career

I worked at Visier for 2 years cumulatively from 2019-2021.

It was an incredible experience. Me and the rest of the small Support team felt like a superhero troupe.

My company supplied laptop and a plate from the weekly office lunch.
For a 20 year old me, this was making it.

At the front-lines of the product, we supported every single Production customer in a myraid of ways:

• We learned Visier's SAAS products inside-out, and advised customers with technical & how-to questions
• We communicated with users on daily basis, logging and prioritizing their feature requests
• We learned the data upload process, and troubleshooted the data UTL process
• We learned the development workflow, and documented bugs in JIRA for the Dev team
• We learned site reliability processes and protocols, and provided 24/7 on-call application uptime support

And much more!





Well, by the start of 2021, I've matured into my Support role at Visier, and I felt like an established professional.

The mental weight of needing to start a stable career, to prove I could make a good living for myself, was lifted from my shoulders.

Now, I could think about a deeper question: what was my career end-goal?

The 10 year old kid spending hours playing with his beige brick of a PC -- what would he want to grow up to be?

The 15 year old teen watching Youtube tutorials to build his own computer -- what would he be proud to do in the future?

The 20 year old college kid who was putting his all into his internship applications -- where did he see himself in 10 years?

The best answer I could give to these questions, was this:

I want to be a technology professional. And I want to be the best one I could be. Maybe even be CTO material some day.

And to be the best tech professional I could be, I knew I'd need to finally learn to code software.

Which brings us to now.





What am I up to now?

I parted ways with Visier in June of 2021.

Thankfully, I was in a position where I had few day-to-day expenses, so I decided I would study full-time

As of writing this (February 2021), I've been self learning programming full-time for 8 months.
More specifically, I've focused on learning object-oriented programming in Python, and web development with HTML + CSS + Javascript.
